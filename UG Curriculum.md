<h2 id="bio101-a-idbio101a">BIO101 <a id="bio101"></a></h2>
<h3 id="introductory-biology">Introductory Biology</h3>
<p><strong>Offered By</strong> : School of Chemical and Materials Sciences<br>
<strong>Approved</strong> : 6<sup>th</sup> Meeting of Senate dated 28 Apr 2020<br>
<strong>Prerequisites</strong> : None</p>
<p><strong>Objective</strong><br>
Nature gives us a glimpse of the most complicated engineering system ever designed. All engineers must be trained to appreciate the aspects of biology where concepts of various phenomena from different fields of engineering like mechanical, chemical, electrical engineering have played a role in understanding the underlying beauty of nature. It is also imperative to understand and address the question of where do we come from and where we are going. This is among the most crucial questions which artificial intelligence thrives upon finding clues from concepts of evolution. The course encourages students to take inspiration from biology to address engineering problems.</p>
<p><strong>Content</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Need for biology</li>
<li>Biomimicry</li>
<li>Engineering solutions inspired from biology</li>
<li>Origin of Life on Earth</li>
<li>Evolution</li>
<li>Mutations</li>
<li>Kingdoms of life</li>
<li>Cell Structure, Cell Communication</li>
<li>Enzyme kinetics</li>
<li>Biomolecules of Life (Lipids, Carbohydrates, Proteins and Nucleic Acids)</li>
<li>Genetic Basis of Development (Central Dogma, Gene Expression), Genetics, Metabolism</li>
<li>Quantitative view of modern biology, and other allied topics.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Textbook(s)</strong><br>
Biology: A global approach, Neil A Campbell et al., 10<sup>th</sup> Edition, 2015, Pearson Education Ltd, England</p>
<p><strong>Reference(s)</strong><br>
None</p>
<h2 id="bo301-a-idbo301a">BO301 <a id="bo301"></a></h2>
<h3 id="introduction-to-bioinformatics">Introduction to Bioinformatics</h3>
<p><strong>Offered by</strong> : School of Interdisciplinary Life Sciences<br>
<strong>Approved</strong> : 19<sup>th</sup> Meeting of Senate dated 15 Apr 2024<br>
<strong>Course Type</strong> : Open Elective<br>
<strong>Offered to</strong> : UG (V &amp; VI semester)<br>
<strong>Prerequisites</strong> : <a href="#bio101">BIO101</a></p>
<p><strong>Objective</strong> :<br>
With the advancement of modern molecular biology techniques, massive genomic and protein databases are available. When properly analyzed, these databases can unveil mysteries of several complex biological phenomena. Manual analysis of such enormous databases is impossible. Therefore, Bioinformatics, an area of biology dealing with the development of new computational techniques to store, process, and analyze large datasets, has emerged as an active research field. This course will give the students an overview of the computational methods used in modern biology research.</p>
<p><strong>Content</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Introduction to Biomacromolecular Databases : Key concepts of protein and nucleic acid sequences, collection, organization, storage, and data retrieval from available databases, an overview of NCBI Entrez and RCSB Protein Databank.</li>
<li>Sequence Alignment : Principles of sequence alignment, Scoring alignments, Types of alignment: Local vs. Global alignment, Pairwise and Multiple Sequence alignment and algorithms, application of sequence alignment.</li>
<li>Evolution of Macromolecular Sequences and Structures : Basis of molecular phylogeny, phylogenetic tree construction.</li>
<li>Structural Bioinformatics : Protein structure basics, protein structure visualization, structural classification of proteins, protein structure comparison, protein structure prediction: introduction to Alphafold.</li>
<li>Introduction to Drug Designing : Proteins as drug targets, the principle of molecular docking.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Textbook(s)</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Introduction to Bioinformatics (5<sup>th</sup> Ed.), Arthur M Lesk, Oxford University Press, 2019</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Reference(s)</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Bioinformatics: Sequence and Genome Analysis (2<sup>nd</sup> Ed.). David W Mount, Cold Spring Harbor Laboratory Press, 2005</li>
<li>Understanding Bioinformatics, Marketa Zvelebil, and Jeremy O. Baum, Garland Science, 2007.</li>
<li>Essential Bioinformatics, Jin Xiong, Cambridge University Press, 2006</li>
<li>Structural Bioinformatics, Jenny Gu, Philip E. Bourne. (2<sup>nd</sup> Ed.). Wiley, 2009.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="cs400-a-idcs400a">CS400 <a id="cs400"></a></h2>
<h3 id="data-structures-and-object-oriented-programming-using-c">Data Structures and Object-Oriented Programming Using C++</h3>
<p><strong>Offered by</strong> : School of Mathematics and Computer Science<br>
<strong>Approved</strong> : 8<sup>th</sup> Meeting of Senate dated 27 Oct 2020<br>
<strong>Course Type</strong>:  Institute Elective - half semester course<br>
<strong>Offered to</strong> : UG (non-CS; Half semester course) 3rd-year and 4th-year students<br>
<strong>Prerequisites</strong> : CS 101 - Introduction to Computer Programming</p>
<p><strong>Objective</strong><br>
This is a fast-paced introductory course on Data Structures and C++ programming constructs. It is not a substitute for an Introduction to Programming Course or Data Structures and Algorithms Course. It is intended for Non-CS students who have completed a first-level programming course in C/Python and want to learn about basic Data Structures. The course will touch upon the concepts and philosophy of object-oriented programming. Students will be graded based on programming assignments and an end-semester examination. There will be no separate lab associated with the course.</p>
<p><strong>Content</strong><br>
<em>Fundamental data structures</em> : Stacks, Queues, Linked List, Trees, Heaps, Graphs, Hash tables Object <em>Orientation Concepts</em> : Data Abstraction, Information hiding, Encapsulation, Inheritance, Polymorphism, Operator Overloading, Dynamic binding, Exception handling, Generic programming Standard Template Library (STL) Components: Containers, Iterators, Algorithms, Iterator Adapters, User-Defined Generic Functions, Function Objects etc. STL Containers: Vectors, Deques, Lists, Sets and Multisets, Maps and Multimaps Special Containers: Stacks, Queues, Priority Queues, Strings</p>
<p><strong>Textbook(s)</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Object-Oriented Programming Using C++ by Ira Phol, Second edition, Addison-Wesley, 1997</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Reference(s)</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Data Structures and Algorithm Analysis in C++ by Mark Allen Weiss, Pearson Education</li>
<li>Data Structure and Algorithm in C++ Paperback by Adam Drozdek, Cengage</li>
<li>C++ Programming Language, by Stroustrup Bjarne, Addison-Wesley</li>
<li>C++ Primer by Lippman Stanley B. Pearson Education</li>
</ul>
